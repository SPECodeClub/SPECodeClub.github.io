Bits

A bit is the littlest unit of information which is stored on a PC framework. There can only be two potential states of a solitary bit: a '1' or a '0'. This is known as a 'boolean'. A 'boolean' or 'bool' for short, can just have two qualities. Consider it a switch: a switch can be one of two things: 'on' or 'off'.

All data in is made up of bits:

1 byte is 8 bits.
1 kilobyte (kB) is 1000 bytes.
1 megabyte (MB) is 1000 kilobytes.
1 gigabyte (GB) is 1000 megabytes.
1 terabyte (TB) is 1000 gigabytes.
4 bits (half a byte) is called a nibble!


Some of you may have read this and thought, that's wrong! It's 1024, not 1000!

Well, there's a story behind that...

We began by using kB to denote 1024 bytes. The problem is that the prefix 'kilo' already means something - 1000. This caused a lot of confusion, so the IEC (International Electro-technical Commission) introduced KiB instead.

That's a kibibyte. There are also mebibytes, gibibytes and tebibytes...

1 kibibyte (KiB) is 1024 bytes.
1 mebibyte (MiB) is 1024 kibibytes.
1 gibibyte (GiB) is 1024 mebibytes.
1 tebibyte (TiB) is 1024 gibibytes.
And so on...

However, this didn't really clear things up for most people. A lot of technical people went on thinking that 1 kB is 1024 bytes, without ever knowing that kibibytes existed.

It still isn't clear when someone writes kB if they mean 1000 bytes or 1024 bytes.
